Mt. Si

12/31/2019 (most recently)

One cannot recreate in Washington without eventually going to Mt. Si. The trail is well-maintained, connects to other long-distance trails, and you can literally take a bus to it from Seattle. I haven’t personally taken that route, but dang would it be cool to get to a hike via Orca card.

In fact, taking the bus is probably the optimal way to get to Mt. Si. The hike’s extreme popularity means that the parking lot, already quite large, fills up in the morning during the peak season. Get there as early as you can (or, like myself, go in the off season).

Anyway, I like to use Si to open the hiking season. The route is the perfect mix of rugged switchbacks and familiarity that makes missing gear less severe while still busting your calves. No wonder this is the most popular hike in the state.

For first-timers, make sure you take some time to look at the interpretive signs roughly a mile into the hike. Mt. Si has some interesting mythology in local Native American cultures and to this day you can still see remnants of wildfires early in the 20th century.

As for sights, the way up is mostly your standard PNW second-growth forest with occasional exposed rock faces. Mt. Si was designated as a state conservation area only in 1977. Before that, mining and logging tore out all of the original scenery.

Proceeding upward, the cedars and douglas firs transition to thinner mountain hemlock forest with occasional peaks through the trees. The first real view point is above some scree next to a heavily-vandalized trail marker. Continue through a short shaded trail before reaching the true destination of the hike: Haystack Rock.

Scrambling up the back side of Haystack is doable (as those who stand atop it can atest), but in my opinion a sketchy scramble isn’t the best way to cap off a hike of Si’s intensity. There’s plenty of space for lunch and pictures, just be careful of squirrels and birds who make a living on hikers’ refuse (remember: no feeding!).

Personally, I’ve been to Si enough that I don’t even take pictures anymore. It’s way more fun taking pictures for people who are summiting for the first time. In summary: get up early for parking, enjoy the switchbacks, and soak in the perfect starter hike on the I-90 corridor.
